Curated by Ella Wynn | August 21 to Decemeber 4, 2025

Contemporary Art in the Modern Political Landscape and Its Influence

This exhibition is a statement of intent, arguing that art is not a passive observer of history but an activator of change, a force against institutional corruption, and a powerful language of transformation.

The Exhibition's Thesis

This exhibition argues that contemporary art does not simply reflect the world's political and social climate. Instead, it influences and molds it. Art is positioned in an engaged, essential role in national dialogue, where criticism is sounded, injustices are confronted, and alternative futures are imagined. By tackling issues like climate change, migration, gender and racial justice, and the precariousness of democracy, these artists show that creativity is not peripheral to politics. It is a driving force.

Moving beyond aesthetic critique, the artists here destabilize institutions of power, subvert dominant narratives, and give voice to marginalized communities. Their work functions as both critique and catalyst, reordering what is seen, said, and felt within a society—a vital reconsideration of what democracy feels like and whom it serves.

Featured Artworks

Agnes Denes

Wheatfield – A Confrontation (1982)

"Wheatfield - A Confrontation" was a profound act of Land Art in which Agnes Denes planted and harvested two acres of golden wheat on a Battery Park landfill, two blocks from Wall Street and the World Trade Center in Manhattan. Commissioned by the Public Art Fund, this ephemeral work took four months to complete, transforming what would become some of the world's most valuable real estate into a powerful rural intrusion. Denes intended the wheat field to symbolize food, energy, commerce, and world hunger in a confrontation with the misplaced priorities of modern society and capitalism. The deliberate contrast between the ancient, life-sustaining crop and the towering symbols of global finance created a striking, unforgettable paradox, and remains Denes's most recognized work.

Hank Willis Thomas

All Power to All People (2017)

This monumental sculpture is an eight-foot-tall, high-gloss black aluminum and stainless steel Afro pick featuring a clenched fist handle. Installed across from City Hall in Philadelphia's Thomas Paine Plaza, the artwork is a powerful public symbol of identity, community, and resistance. The Afro pick itself, an accessory with roots in ancient Egypt, became a potent emblem of the 1970s Black Power Movement and a signature of collective identity. By scaling this common object to the size of a public monument, Thomas prompts dialogue about whose histories and symbols are represented in civic spaces, invoking the civil rights rallying call that inspired the work's title.

Nicole Eisenman

Morning Affirmations (2018)

A perfect example of Eisenman's use of allegory and contemporary satire, Morning Affirmations portrays a cartoonish Bald Eagle, the nation's bird and a symbol of the United States, standing in front of a bathroom mirror while involved in self-help. He is brushing his teeth, no doubt repeating positive, daily affirmations. The work wittily satirizes the national ego and the political landscape of post-truth, betraying a vulnerable, insecure, yet self-aggrandizing America. Eisenman employs humor with a bold, expressive style to comment on the cultural anxieties and political climate of the late 2010s, exposing a feeling of forced optimism in the face of national decline.

Nicole Eisenman

The Triumph of Poverty (2009)

This large-scale, allegorical painting responds directly to the global financial crisis of the late 2000s, referencing Pieter Bruegel the Elder’s 1568 masterpiece, The Blind Leading the Blind. Eisenman's canvas features a motley, bedraggled crew of modern figures, representing the newly impoverished and disenfranchised, trudging across a bleak landscape toward an unknown fate. This procession is accompanied by a top-hatted, tuxedo-wearing man, perhaps a symbol of misguided leadership or financial elite, moving "ass-backward." The work is a poignant and darkly humorous critique of capitalism's failures, depicting the financial "triumph of poverty" as a collective, absurd, and blind march into economic uncertainty.

Banksy

I Love New York; Don't We All? (2008)

This piece forms part of a high-profile series Banksy executed in New York City in 2008 and is well-known for its biting, site-specific humor. The most famous version featured the phrase "I love New York; don't we all?" stenciled above a rat carrying a sign that read "THIS IS MY NEW YORK." Another iteration involved a quote on a garbage can stencil. Details change, but the central theme remains the same: it's a satire about commercialization and the often shallow world love for New York City. Banksy takes this slogan of a beloved tourist and juxtaposes it with his own motif-the ubiquitous, anti-establishment rat-to critique the glossy veneer of the city against the backdrop of often-overlooked gritty realities and the "vermin" who populate its streets.

Banksy

Fuck the Police (2000)

This early studio work embodies Banksy's raw, anti-authoritarian stance. It depicts a police officer, rendered in the artist’s signature black-and-white stencil style, tightly gripping his baton and staring intensely just beyond the frame. Behind the officer, the titular phrase, “Fuck the police,” is scrawled in crude red letters, suggesting the officer has arrived too late to catch the vandal, leaving only the brazen, mocking slogan. The work’s simple, stark visual language and confrontation with law enforcement are hallmarks of Banksy's practice, linking his street art to the sentiments of hip-hop culture and reflecting the artist’s own adversarial relationship with police as a graffiti writer.

Nicole Eisenman

Hanging Birth (1994)

Hanging Birth is an early, highly symbolic figurative painting by Eisenman. It shows a scene of a stark, ritualistic intensity: a woman hangs by a noose around her neck from a tree, while a circle of solemn men is standing beneath her, ready to receive the baby being born into their hands. This is a disturbing tableau that explores themes of female sacrifice, creation hurt, and the way male control is imposed upon women's bodies and their powers of reproduction. The work's composition and surreal, almost grotesque figures reflect Eisenman's early engagement with identity, trauma, and social burdens placed upon women, delivered with a mix of dark humor and expressive, raw painting technique.

Nicole Eisenman

Tea Party (2011)

Eisenman's Tea Party is a pointed political allegory critiquing the American conservative political movement of the same name. The painting features three central figures, often interpreted as a revolutionary, a businessman, and a skeleton, standing in a shallow pool of water. They hold a makeshift banner/weapon that is part American flag and part scythe, topped with a pineapple, a symbol of colonial hospitality. Eisenman uses surreal, physical details, such as an anus-shaped ear on one figure, to suggest that the group is deaf to outside reason. The work effectively portrays the movement as a grotesque, isolated, and politically self-sabotaging force operating outside the mainstream.

The Secret Handshake

Statue of Trump and Epstein Holding Hands (2025)

This is a covert and mercurial art installation by the anonymous collective The Secret Handshake, a piece of direct political action art. It consists of a bronze-colored statue depicting Donald Trump and Jeffrey Epstein holding hands, kicking up their legs in an image of shared merriment. Often placed without authorization into major public spaces-the National Mall, for example-before being removed, the aim is to generate immediate media attention and public debate, satirically "celebrating" the documented association between the two figures while criticizing the powerful institutions that shield them. The sculpture's ephemerality and guerrilla nature are key to its message, prioritizing political critique over traditional permanence.

Daniel J. Martinez

Museum Tags (1993)

For the 1993 Whitney Biennial, Daniel J. Martinez exchanged the standard admission tags for buttons emblazoned with a most provocative and politicized statement: "I can't imagine ever wanting to be white." This intervention, entitled Museum Tags: Second Movement (overture) or Overture con claque, made every museum viewer an active, public wearer of a categorical denial of racial privilege, willing or not. The work was a challenge in itself to the largely white institutional structures of the art world and to the very notion of racial identity in America, taking the museum's own audience as an unwitting yet integral part of the piece itself.

Meet the Artists

Agnes Denes

Conceptual Artist, Land Art Pioneer

Agnes Denes was born in 1931 in Hungary and is a seminal figure in conceptual and environmental art. She attended the New School and Columbia University, both in New York, NY. She has been a pioneer in ecological concerns since the late 1960s. In her practice, which she calls "Visual Philosophy," the artist interlinks disciplines like science, mathematics, philosophy, and poetry to examine global problems of humankind and the environment. Her legendary "Wheatfield - A Confrontation" (1982) included planting and harvesting two acres of wheat on a Manhattan landfill near Wall Street-a powerful statement on food shortage, land use, and capitalism. Denes's large-scale earthworks, drawings, and prints reflect her deep commitment to unifying the human intellect with nature.

Hank Willis Thomas

Conceptual Artist, Photographer

Hank Willis Thomas (b. 1976, Plainfield, New Jersey) is a conceptual artist based in Brooklyn who works across photography, sculpture, and installation to explore themes of racial identity, history, and the commodification of Black bodies in American media and popular culture. Thomas attended Duke Ellington School of the Arts as a Museum Studies student. Often appropriating recognizable imagery, mostly from advertising, to expose the underlying racial and gender biases, his provocative series includes B®anded, which superimposes the Nike swoosh onto the bodies of Black men, and Unbranded, which digitally strips ads of all text and logos. In 2016, Thomas cofounded the artist-led initiative For Freedoms, a platform for creative civic engagement and deepening public conversations about freedom in the 21st century.

Nicole Eisenman

Painter, Satirist

French-born American artist Nicole Eisenman (b. 1965) is recognized for her expressive and intricate oil paintings and sculptures, which return the human figure to a pertinent position within contemporary culture. She studied at the Rhode Island School of Design. Often fusing classic painting styles with aspects of comic art and black humor, her work covers topics such as gender, queer identity, community, and the human condition in a very personal and socio-political manner. Eisenman's figures are often set in scenes that are both absurd and poignant, speaking to the intricacies of modern life. They are a recipient of the prestigious MacArthur Fellowship and have been widely exhibited, including multiple inclusions in the Whitney Biennial.

Banksy

Graffiti Artist, Political Activist (Anonymous)

Banksy is the pseudonymous, England-based street artist, political activist, and film director whose real name remains unknown. Active since the 1990s, Banksy rose to international fame for his satirical street art and subversive epigrams executed in a distinctive stenciling technique. His works, marrying dark humor and political or social commentary, have surfaced unexpectedly on walls, bridges, and public spaces around the world, addressing anti-war movements, anti-capitalism, and anti-establishment themes. Banksy's strategic anonymity and high-profile self-shredding painting at auction have created a global icon of guerrilla art activism.

The Secret Handshake

Mysterious Collective

The Secret Handshake (TSH) is an anonymous, covert art collective primarily focused on political art and activism. Operating with what they term a "Hybrid Model of Covert Art Activism," TSH utilizes guerrilla-style interventions, staged symbolism, and calculated narrative management, often trading artistic permanence for maximum political utility. Their work gained significant attention for its high-profile, unauthorized installations in politically sensitive locations, targeting powerful figures and institutions with pointed critique. The collective's anonymity is central to their strategy, deflecting attention from the artists and focusing it squarely on the politically charged message of their ephemeral, conceptually functional art.

Daniel J. Martinez

Conceptual & Installation Artist

Daniel J. Martinez, born in 1957, is a conceptual artist and educator based in Los Angeles whose multidisciplinary practice ranges from installation, robotics, and photography to public intervention. Martinez deals with the sociopolitical fault lines within American society by navigating issues of race and identity, and the tension between political activism and personal culpability. He gained critical attention for his controversial intervention into the 1993 Whitney Biennial, where he replaced the museum tags with badges reading, "I can't imagine ever wanting to be white." His provocative art challenges systems of language and representation through which power and identity are defined.

The Full Statement

Contemporary Art in the Modern Political Landscape and Its Influence is not only an exhibition, but a statement of intent. It argues that contemporary art does not simply reflect the world's political and social climate. Instead, it influences and molds it. In so doing, art becomes an activator of change and a force against institutional corruption. The show demolishes the mythology of art as a passive observer of culture and history. It positions art in an engaged, essential role in national dialogue. Here, criticism is sounded, injustices are confronted, and alternative futures are imagined. Through diverse artworks addressing systemic racism, environmental destruction, gender discrimination, and the abuse of power, this exhibition argues that art is one of the strongest languages of protest and transformation in society.

The exhibition's argument depends on the belief that art can reflect and define the political consciousness of its time. Today's artists, the show insists, are not content to merely represent or document social crises. Rather, they destabilize institutions of power, subvert dominant narratives, and give voice to marginalized communities. By tackling issues like climate change, migration, gender and racial justice, and the precariousness of American democracy, these artists show that creativity is not peripheral to politics. It is a driving force. Their practice recalls how aesthetic space structures collective imagination, mobilizes empathy, and catalyzes civic action.

The political discourse in the United States today is marked by extreme polarization, widespread corruption, and pervasive distrust of democratic institutions. It requires an equally robust cultural and artistic response. The artists in this exhibition fill that need. They move beyond aesthetic critique to engage in public debate. Their work does not merely reflect reality—it remakes it. They show us how art functions as both critique and catalyst, fostering an interdependent exchange between political power and creative expression. Political events frame artistic creation, but art can frame public perception and even influence policy. This active interaction recalls theorist Jacques Rancière's notion of the "distribution of the sensible."

Finally, the show works as an act of provocation. It seeks not just to present art, but to energize onlookers. Its central argument is that contemporary art can read, comment on, and invert current political and social issues, and act as a major force for change. This idea is reflected in the work of cultural critic bell hooks, who felt that art could be a "space of radical possibility." Through making art that is oppositional to domination and proactive in defending human dignity, current artists offer visions of freedom that are not limited to the museum space. The show is then no longer a reflection of political reality, but an act of political imagination—a space where the public can envision more equitable futures.
